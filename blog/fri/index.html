The FRI Protocol

Low-Degree Testing in Logarithmic Time

PUBLISHED ON NOV 11, 2024

Notation

Let $\mathbb F$ denote a finite field.

In essence, the Fast Reed-Solomon Interactive Oracle Proof of Proximity (FRI) is a statistical test which enables a verifier to check that a given (univariate) polynomial $P(X)$ over $\mathbb F$ is of low-degree (i.e., doesn’t exceed a certain degree bound $k$)1.

A verifier can naively check the claim $\deg(P)<k$ by querying the evaluations of $P$ on any $k$ points of $\mathbb F$, interpolating a polynomial $P’$ over those $k$ points and check if $P(a)=P’(a)$ for an additional random point $a\in\mathbb F$. Thus, such a naive verifier needs $\mathcal{O}(k)$ queries and $\mathcal{O}(k\log k)$ time (more on that later). The strength of FRI lies in its ability to check the same claim with only $\mathcal{O}(\log k)$ queries and in $\mathcal{O}(\log k)$ time (more on that later as well).

Rephrased in the language of Interactive Oracle Proofs (IOP) and codes, FRI is an IOP for proving that a given function is “close” to a Reed-Solomon codeword. Here, “close” refers to the concept of proximity in terms of the (relative) Hamming distance, and a Reed-Solomon codeword corresponds to the evaluation of a low-degree polynomial over a suitable domain. Due to the sublinear verification time (and query complexity) of FRI, it is inherently impossible to prove exact identity with a low-degree polynomial (i.e., a Reed-Solomon codeword), but only proximity to such a polynomial. It would, thus, be more precise to say that

“FRI is a statistical test which enables a verifier to check (in logarithmic time) if a given polynomial is close to a low-degree polynomial.”

Leaving the (much more challenging) soundness analysis aside for the moment, the completeness property of FRI states: a codeword $u\in\text{RS}[\mathbb F,D^{(0)},\rho]$ always passes the checks by the verifier. The completeness of FRI relies on the implication $$ f^{(0)}\in\text{RS}[\mathbb F,D^{(0)},\rho] \Longrightarrow f^{(1)}\in\text{RS}[\mathbb F,D^{(1)},\rho]. % f^{(0)}\in\text{RS}[\mathbb F,D^{(0)},\rho] \stackrel{w.h.c.}{\Longleftrightarrow} f^{(1)}\in\text{RS}[\mathbb F,D^{(1)},\rho]. % \Delta\left(f^{(0)},\text{RS}[\mathbb F,D^{(0)},\rho]\right)<\delta \stackrel{w.h.c.}{\Longleftrightarrow} \Delta\left(f^{(1)},\text{RS}[\mathbb F,D^{(1)},\rho]\right)<\delta. $$
